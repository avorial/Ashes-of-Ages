## Background

Born in the Arabian Peninsula in 985 AD, Yahya al-Qamar was a Bedouin, traveling across the deserts of Arabia as a devout Muslim. He followed the Prophet's teachings closely and became known for his wisdom and knowledge of the faith. As a wanderer, he found himself welcomed in the courts of the Caliphate and noble households of Al-Andalus and the Middle East, where he shared his insights as both a spiritual advisor and a scholar. His knowledge of theology and philosophy made him respected, but it also stirred in him a deeper hunger for understanding the divine, one that led him on a fateful journey to Constantinople in the early 11th century.

In 1008, while in Constantinople, Yahya encountered a Malkavian elder who was captivated by his fervent search for truth and the divine. This elder, seeing a kindred spirit in Yahya's endless quest to understand God, decided to Embrace him, believing that immortality would allow Yahya to delve deeper into his search for divine knowledge. The Embrace, however, shattered Yahya's world. While it granted him the immortality to pursue wisdom, it also fractured his mind. His insight deepened, but so did his paranoia and delusions. Yahya soon had a religious revelation that caused him to convert to Christianity, believing that Christ, rather than the Prophet, held the ultimate key to understanding God's plan.

After his conversion, Yahya joined a Christian monastery, thinking that the monastic life would bring him closer to divine revelation. He spent years, decades even, in quiet contemplation, studying scripture and trying to understand the mysteries of God. However, instead of bringing him peace, this life only deepened his paranoia. He began to believe that God was constantly sending him messages--signs and visions--but that he was failing to interpret them correctly. This fear gnawed at him, driving him to the edge of madness. Yahya became obsessed with deciphering these divine messages, convinced that he was part of some grand, cosmic plan but eternally unsure of his role within it.

To quell his paranoia, Yahya began embracing individuals who claimed to have prophetic abilities--seers, mystics, and visionaries--hoping that through them, he could find the clarity he so desperately sought. These childer were meant to act as conduits between him and God, interpreting the messages that Yahya believed were being sent his way. Yet, every decade or so, the revelations they brought him would fail to satisfy his ever-growing need for understanding. Each failure drove him deeper into madness, as his belief that he was missing some crucial aspect of the divine plan consumed him. He would abandon his childer, moving on to find new prophets to embrace, but none could grant him the answers he sought.

In 1430, feeling once again restless and unfulfilled, Yahya traveled north, leaving behind the monasteries of Byzantium to seek out new forms of wisdom among the witches and seers of the Slavic lands. He believed that the magic and ancient traditions of these lands might offer him a new perspective on God's messages. However, as he wandered through Bohemia, Yahya stumbled upon a **Hussite communion**, a gathering that would change his path once again. In their radical reinterpretation of Christian doctrine and their rebellion against the Catholic Church, Yahya saw echoes of the divine confusion he felt. The Hussites, with their rejection of established authority, resonated with Yahya's belief that God's truth was being misinterpreted and that the established order was blind to the true messages from above.

Convinced that Prague, the heart of the Hussite movement, was where God intended him to be, Yahya settled in the city in 1433. He became fascinated with the Hussites' interpretation of Christian scripture, feeling that perhaps their struggle was the key to unlocking the mystery of God's messages. He interpreted the Hussite rebellion as a sign that Prague was the epicenter of a divine plan he had to uncover. His paranoia deepened once more, as he believed the city itself held the answers he had been searching for across centuries. The religious upheaval and the tensions between Catholicism and the Hussites fed into his belief that this was the battleground where God's true intentions would finally be revealed to him.

To aid in his search, Yahya has begun calling his scattered childer back to Prague. He believes that together, they can piece together the signs and messages that have plagued him for so long. However, many of his childer, having endured decades or even centuries of his madness, are wary of returning. Those who do answer his summons find a Yahya more paranoid and obsessed than ever, convinced that his role in God's plan is on the verge of being revealed. They are drawn into his world of prophetic visions, religious zeal, and divine confusion, as Yahya continues his relentless quest to uncover the truth hidden behind God's cryptic messages.

## Disciplines and powers

- **Auspex** (0)
  Sudden Revelations
  
  Characters with even one level of Auspex may occasionally get a sense of an impending threat against them. While they are otherwise using any level of Auspex, the Storyteller may choose to roll for a sudden revelation. In secret, she rolls the character's Auspex score. The difficulty varies by Storyteller discretion, as a robber behind an inn door would probably be difficulty 4, while a plot by the Prince's childe to destroythe character that would take decades to unravel would be difficulty 9. Difficulty can also reflect how important it is to the story that the character has a glimpse of what's coming. Keep in mind though, it is only a brief precognitive glimpse that may or may not make sense before the event unfurls. Divination is not a science; express the revelation in small sensory details or simple gut feelings. "You smell fire with no source coming from the farm house" or "he can't be trusted alone" are excellent examples
- **Obfuscate** (0)
- **Dementation** (0)
  If madness shatters the soul, Dementation is the hammer to the fracture point. If madness frees the mind, Dementation is the key to the cage. The Discipline allows a vampire to focus insanity and channel it to those around them. Malkavian clan elders claim the conscious mind is a pleasing and soothing mask, and that the Discipline never creates a madness that wasn't already there. Instead, it crumbles the barriers of self-control and consciousness to unleash the deep, dark insanity that dwells beneath. Viewed in this light, Dementation does not corrupt the mind ofthe victim--it unmasks their mind's truth for all to see.
  
  Each level of Dementation may only be used once per night on a victim. While Malkavians are the natural masters of this Discipline, it's usable by any vampire who possesses a Derangement. Vampires learning Dementation will gradually develop a Derangement; if this Derangementis cured by means magical or mundane, their knowledge of Dementation becomes unusable academia. Dwelling on this knowledge presages a relapse into madness.
- **Heightened Senses** (1)
  This power costs nothing, is reflexive, and requires no rolls to use. When activated, any roll related to the sense's use is decreased in difficulty by the character's Auspex dots. A character may utilize one sense at a time, or all of them in any combination she likes.This power does not allow the character to see in perfect darkness as with Eyes of the Beast (see p. 240).
- **Read the Soul** (2)
  The soul-gazer must actively and attentively observe her subject, allowing the sense of the soul to wash over her. At the moment she chooses to interpret the state of the soul, the character's player rolls Perception + Empathy (difficulty 8), with each success granting the character more pieces of information about their subject. Consider the chart below for ideas as to what information the Storyteller might communicate.
  
  A failure indicates that the character was unable to interpret the current state of the soul. A botch indicates misinformation or a wrong interpretation. Soul-gazing is not a science.The chart on the right consists of colors and their suggested interpretations. Interpretations through other senses are also possible. Players and Storytellers should be encouraged to create charts for their characters, unique in its sensory details.
  
  1 success Can distinguish only the shade (pale orbright).
  
  2 successes  Can distinguish the main color.
  
  3 successes  Can recognize the color patterns.
  
  4 successes  Can detect subtle shifts.
  
  5 successes  Can identify mixtures of color and pattern.
  
  The ability to Read the Soul does not act as a lie-detector, and absolute truths cannot be determined. It just reveals moments of dishonesty, or flooded feelings of guilt. An exceptional liar may be free of guilt, and a penitent but innocent person may always appear on the edge ofsuspicion. A talented Auspex user knows this, and will keep it in mind when interpreting what she perceives.
  
  An Auspex user may use Read the Soul to search a crowd or scan an area. She starts out by declaring what she is looking for, such as "a soul stained with Amaranth" or "the most nervous person in the room." Her player then rolls as normal. The information is general, and anymore intimate study of a target's soul requires a new roll
- **Spirit's Touch** (3)
  A player rolls Perception + Empathy. Difficulty varies by age and strength of impression. A stake found in a pile of ash just an hour old may be difficulty 4, a stake that had been in a vampire killed five days ago might be a difficulty 6, and a stake that only carried intent but was never used may yield useful impressions only at a difficulty 9.
  
  Difficulty should only be considered when a challenge and possible failure would add to the story. Sometimes,a Storyteller might choose to forgo a roll entirely when acharacter attempts to touch an object that is vital to the continuing story or a piece of strong setting importance. If the Storyteller needs characters to know they have a fang pulled from a Methuselah's mouth, or the sword that decapitated the Sheriff, she should forgo the roll and bring on the terrifying visions.
  
  1 success  Very basic information: the last owner'sgender and hair color, for instance.
  
  2 successes  A second piece of basic information.
  
  3 successes  More useful information about the last owner, such as age and state of mind the last time he used the item.
  
  4 successes  The person's name.
  
  5+ successes  A wealth of information is available; nearly anything you want to know about theperson's relationship with that object.

- **Eyes of Chaos** (3)
  This power may only be used when scrutinizing a complex pattern or event, such as the interactions of a vampiric court or the clash of an army. Analyzing the blood spatter flicked from one's fingers or the spill of entrails works just as well, however. Roll Perception + Enigmas; the difficulty varies based on available information and the vampire's familiarity with the situation. A court in the vampire's home cultureand domain might be difficulty 5, while a foreign court (or the clash of two foreign armies) is difficulty 7. A completely alien encounter (encountering one of the mysterious Cathayan vampires, or even just meeting an Anda) would be difficulty 9. The player asks the Storyteller a direct question about the situation at hand. This can be virtually anything related to hidden truths. The answers are related to the player directly and to the character via metaphors and imagery, with more successes providing a clearer and more complete answer. Failure produces imagery unrelated tothe matter at hand, while a botch on the roll relates a completely untrue but nevertheless believable answer, which may explain some of the more radical Malkavian behavior. Viable questions include, but are not limited to:
  
  &bull;What's this person's Nature and Derangements?
  
  &bull;What's the worst choice I could make?
  
  &bull;What's the safest choice?
  
  &bull;Is this person being controlled by another?
  
  &bull;What's the underlying or hidden message here?
  
  &bull;How relevant to our overarching goals is this scene?
- **Fire Voice** (4)
  This power is a focused, more potent SoulHaunting. The vampire still rolls Manipulation + Empathy (difficulty 7); success on the roll now allows the vampire to affect one target per success, although all potential victims must still be listening to the vampire's voice for three turns. Affected victims immediately fly into a R&ouml;tschreck-like blind fear. Vampires (or other creatures capable of frenzy, such as lupines) may make a R&ouml;tschreck test (Storyteller's choice as to how others are affected) at +2 difficulty to resist the power. Mortals are automatically affected and don't remember their actions while frightened. The frenzy or fear lasts for a scene, with the Soul Haunting effect lasting for an additional night thereafter (and with especially fiery imagery). Vampires and others may test as usual to break their frenzy.
  
  The vampire using Fire Voice must also test for R&ouml;tschreck upon invoking this power, though his difficulty to resist is one lower than normal. If the initial roll to invoke this power is a failure, however, the difficulty of the roll to resist the frenzy increases by one instead. If the roll to invoke this power is a botch, the R&ouml;tschreck response is automatic.

  The vampire must gain her target's undivided attention for at least one full turn to enact this power. The player spends a blood point and rolls Manipulation + Intimidation (difficulty of her victim's current Willpower points). If the roll is successful, the victim is afflicted with five Derangements -- a Lunacy Derangement and the Four Humor imbalances. The number of successes determines the duration.
  
  1 success  One turn
  
  2 successes  One night
  
  3 successes  One week
  
  4 successes  One month
  
  5+ successesOne year
  
  On a botch, the vampire suffers all of the Derangements intended for the victim. A minimum of six Derangements may well reduce the vampire to catatonic torpor.The victim (or the target of a botch) can spend a number of Willpower points equal to the successes rolled to end the duration prematurely.
- **The Silence of Death** (1)
  There is no roll. The power creates a zone of absolute silence around the character, with a maximum radius of 7 meters. No sound occurs inside this zone, though sounds originating outside the area of effect maybe heard by anyone in it. Additionally, if the player spends two blood points and the character spends five turns inconcentration in a room or enclosure, the silence remainsin it after the character leaves the room and lasts until she wills it to end. A character can't create more than one zone of silence, so if she wishes to use Silence of Death on herself (or curse another room), the zone in the first room fades automatically.
